##  Bag of Words (

### **Core Idea**

Represent each document as a vector of word counts (or presence), ignoring grammar and order.

---

### **How it Works (cleaned + corrected)**

1. Convert text to lowercase
2. Tokenize (split into words)
3. Remove stopwords *(optional but common)*
4. (Optional) apply stemming/lemmatization
5. Build a **vocabulary** of unique words from the corpus
6. Convert each document into a vector based on that vocabulary:

   * count occurrences (normal BOW)
   * or mark presence (binary BOW)

👉 Small correction:

* You **don’t have to sort by frequency** unless you're limiting vocabulary size
* “Top K words” is an **optimization step**, not a core step

---

### **Types**

* **Binary BOW**

  * Only checks presence (0 or 1)

* **Count (Normal) BOW**

  * Stores actual frequency of words

---

### **Example (simple intuition)**

Corpus:

* “I love NLP”
* “I love coding”

Vocabulary:
`[I, love, NLP, coding]`

Vectors:

* “I love NLP” → `[1, 1, 1, 0]`
* “I love coding” → `[1, 1, 0, 1]`

---

##  Advantages (refined)

1. Simple and easy to implement
2. Fixed-length vector → easy for ML models
3. Works surprisingly well for basic tasks
4. Faster than advanced methods

---

##  Disadvantages

### 1) Sparse Matrix Problem

* Most values are 0
* Leads to:

  * higher memory usage
  * slower computation

---

### 2) No Word Order (VERY IMPORTANT)

* “dog bites man”
* “man bites dog”

👉 Same vector → meaning lost

---

### 3) No Context / Semantics

* “good” vs “not good”
  👉 Treated almost the same

---

### 4) Vocabulary Limitation (slight correction to your point)

* New unseen words at test time → ignored
* Model cannot understand them

*(It doesn’t “fail,” but it loses information)*

---

### 5) High Dimensionality

* Vocabulary can be huge → thousands of features

---

##  Key Insight (important)

Bag of Words is not “wrong”
It’s just **too shallow for modern NLP problems**

It works when:

* text is simple
* vocabulary is limited
* context isn’t critical

---
